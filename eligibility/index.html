Subchapter V Eligibility — The Debt-Ceiling Math

Sub V eligibility is governed by 11 U.S.C. § 101(51D), which limits Sub V to small-business debtors with aggregate noncontingent liquidated debt below a statutory ceiling. This page covers the math that drives in/out of Sub V, the post-CARES sunset, and traps to watch for.

The current ceiling (post-sunset)

As of June 21, 2024 (sunset date), the Sub V debt ceiling reverted to the original SBRA threshold, COLA-adjusted. The current figure (as of the most recent COLA adjustment) is approximately $3,024,725.

Cases filed before June 21, 2024 used the CARES Act ceiling of $7,500,000. Cases filed on or after that date use the lower threshold.

What counts in the calculation

The ceiling counts noncontingent liquidated debts, both secured and unsecured. Some practical notes:

  • Mortgage debt on the debtor's principal residence: excluded (per § 101(51D)(B)(i))
  • Disputed debt: if genuinely disputed (not just objected to), generally not counted
  • Contingent debt: not counted unless the contingency is satisfied
  • Affiliates: debt of affiliates aggregates with the debtor's debt for ceiling purposes (this is a major trap for franchise operators)

The "engaged in commercial or business activities" test

Sub V also requires that the debtor be "engaged in commercial or business activities." This is a low bar but does exclude:

  • Pure consumer debtors with no business operations
  • Single-asset real estate debtors (separately governed by SARE rules)
  • Debtors who closed the business years before filing (with no remaining commercial activity)

50% test for individual debtors

Individual debtors face an additional hurdle: at least 50% of the debt must arise from commercial or business activities (not consumer debt). Personal credit cards, medical debt, and student loans don't count toward this fraction.

Common eligibility traps

  • Tax debt: personal income tax may push an otherwise-eligible debtor over the ceiling. Carve out separately.
  • Personal guarantees: if the debtor signed PGs on multiple business loans, those PGs are personal debt that count toward the ceiling.
  • Affiliates and successor liability: if the small business has affiliates with their own debt, aggregation can blow the ceiling.
  • Contingent debt becoming liquidated: a guaranty that's contingent at filing but liquidated by judgment soon after can change the eligibility analysis post-petition.
