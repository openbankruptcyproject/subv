Subchapter V Cramdown — The Modified Absolute Priority Rule

Sub V's most distinctive feature is its modification of the absolute priority rule. Equity owners can retain their interests even over creditor objection, provided the plan satisfies Sub V's "fair and equitable" test. This page covers how the rule actually works and where it diverges from standard Chapter 11.

Standard Chapter 11 absolute priority

In standard Chapter 11, § 1129(b) imposes the "absolute priority rule": a class of unsecured creditors that votes against the plan and is impaired must be paid in full before any junior class (including equity) retains anything. The classic exception is "new value" — equity contributing new money in exchange for a continuing interest.

Sub V's modification

Sub V replaces this with a different test under § 1191(c). For a Sub V plan to be confirmed without acceptance by all impaired classes, the court must find:

  1. The plan does not unfairly discriminate, and is fair and equitable, with respect to each impaired class that has not accepted the plan;
  2. The plan provides that all of the debtor's projected disposable income for 3-5 years (at court's discretion) will be used to make plan payments;
  3. The court finds there is a reasonable likelihood the debtor will be able to make all payments under the plan;
  4. The plan provides appropriate remedies, which may include the liquidation of nonexempt assets, in the event of default.

Critically, the absolute priority rule of § 1129(b)(2)(B) is NOT incorporated. Equity owners can retain their interests as long as the disposable-income / fair-and-equitable test is met.

Why this matters

Sub V cramdown is the small-business equivalent of Chapter 13's repayment-plan-and-keep-property structure. The owner stays at the helm, creditors get paid from disposable income over the plan period, and at the end of the plan the remaining unsecured debt is discharged.

This is fundamentally different from standard Chapter 11, where equity wipeout is the default in cramdown unless creditors consent or new value is contributed.

The "disposable income" calculation

Disposable income for Sub V purposes is defined in § 1191(d) by reference to § 1325(b)(2): income received by the debtor that is not reasonably necessary to be expended for maintenance and support of the debtor and dependents, or for business operations.

For corporate debtors, the calculation focuses on operating income net of necessary expenses for continued operations. For individual debtors, the calculation tracks consumer Ch.13 disposable-income analysis.

Plan duration discretion

The court has discretion to set the plan period at 3, 4, or 5 years. Courts typically default to 5 years for cramdown cases, on the theory that creditor recovery should match the longer Sub V duration permitted under the statute.
