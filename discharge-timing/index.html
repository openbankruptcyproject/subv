Subchapter V Discharge Timing — § 1192 and the Doctrinal Split

Sub V discharge timing is the hottest doctrinal area in small-business bankruptcy practice. The statute splits discharge between confirmation (consensual plans) and plan completion (cramdown plans), and courts have split on how the timing rules interact with non-dischargeability under § 523.

The statutory framework

Section 1192 of the Code provides:

  • § 1192: If the plan is confirmed under § 1191(b) (cramdown), the debtor's discharge is granted at plan completion (after disposable income is paid in for the plan period).
  • § 1141(d): If the plan is confirmed under § 1191(a) (consensual), discharge is at confirmation.

The discharge-scope question — the doctrinal split

Section 1192 also says the discharge issued at plan completion in a cramdown case "discharges the debtor from any debt—" with two exceptions: debts the plan discharges by its terms, and debts of a kind specified in § 523(a) on which a timely complaint was filed.

The question that has split courts: does § 523(a)'s exceptions to discharge apply to corporate debtors in Sub V cramdown? Or only to individual debtors?

  • Cantwell-Cleary (4th Cir. 2024): § 523(a) does NOT apply to corporate Sub V cramdown debtors — the discharge under § 1192 is broader for entities than individuals.
  • GFS Industrial / Spring Valley (5th Cir. 2024): § 523(a) DOES apply to corporate Sub V cramdown debtors — the § 1192 cross-reference incorporates § 523(a) regardless of debtor type.
  • 9th, 10th Circuits and others: have come down on different sides; the split is real and unresolved.

Why this matters

For a corporate Sub V cramdown debtor with debt arising from fraud (§ 523(a)(2)), defalcation (§ 523(a)(4)), or willful and malicious injury (§ 523(a)(6)):

  • Under Cantwell-Cleary: the discharge wipes the slate clean, including those debts
  • Under GFS Industrial: those debts survive discharge and remain collectable from the corporate debtor post-confirmation

The split matters most for corporate debtors with significant pre-petition fraud claims against them — the Sub V plan structure either does or does not give them a fresh start, depending on which line of authority controls.

Local practice notes

Courts in the Eighth Circuit (D. Minn., E.D. Mo., W.D. Mo.) have generally followed an analytical framework that tracks the GFS Industrial reasoning, applying § 523(a) to corporate Sub V debtors. Judges in those districts may apply the McGill-line analysis at confirmation. Practitioners should expect doctrine to shift as the Supreme Court eventually addresses the split.

Practical guidance

  • For corporate Sub V cases with potential fraud claims: pre-confirmation negotiation with affected creditors is critical, regardless of which authority controls.
  • For individual Sub V cases: § 523(a) clearly applies; standard non-dischargeability practice governs.
  • Plan-discharge timing should be drafted explicitly into the plan to minimize post-confirmation disputes.
